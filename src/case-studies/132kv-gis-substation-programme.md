## The situation

Lockrose was appointed by a regional electricity distribution network operator to help manage a portfolio of critical infrastructure schemes. Those schemes included three 132kV GIS substations needed to expand network capacity, support growing customer connections, and reinforce the network as demand increased through electrification and renewable generation.

At the centre of that portfolio was a delayed 132kV GIS substation redevelopment. Despite customer acceptance, the project had remained in options assessment for more than two years. Progress had stalled, and the knock-on effect threatened wider programme activities.

## The pressure

The challenge was not a single issue. It was a combination of technical, regulatory, commercial, and land-related constraints all sitting on the same project at the same time.

### Delayed project start

The scheme needed to move quickly out of prolonged initiation and into a delivery route the client could act on with confidence.

### Technical integration

The redevelopment involved introducing non-SF₆ 132kV GIS switchgear using clean gas technology. Delivery was being pursued through a User Self Build arrangement, which meant the design and compliance process also had to align with national electricity transmission standards.

### Complex land acquisition

The project needed both permanent and temporary land rights over a five-year period. Ownership was split across several parties, including a local authority, a utility provider, a tenant farmer operating under a farm business tenancy agreement, and the network operator itself. Legal delays carried a real risk of holding up critical path activities.

## What Lockrose did

Lockrose deployed a senior-led programme management team to take control of delivery and create early momentum across the critical issues.

### Drove the options assessment to a decision

Lockrose led a rigorous options assessment and design review, helping the client move to a preferred solution that was approved within four weeks. A phased contracting strategy was then put in place so early site works could progress in parallel with final design development.

### Managed technical interfaces

Targeted technical studies were commissioned to make sure the GIS technology could meet operational and integration requirements. Lockrose also helped streamline the compliance process with national transmission standards, reducing interface delays and limiting long-lead procurement risk.

### Unblocked land and stakeholder issues

Lockrose negotiated directly with stakeholders and proposed practical solutions, including land swap arrangements, to overcome legal barriers. Temporary access to key land parcels was secured under farm business tenancy agreements, enabling construction compounds and early mobilisation activity to move forward. Planning and environmental consents were also supported through active coordination with the relevant authorities.
